Arithmetic Mean

The arithmetic mean is the sum of all values divided by their count. Ideal for additive processes, but sensitive to outliers.

Example: \( \bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i \)    e.g. {2, 4, 6} → \( \frac{2 + 4 + 6}{3} = 4 \)

Geometric Mean

The nth root of the product of positive values. Essential for growth and compounding.

Example: \( G = \sqrt[n]{x_1 \cdot x_2 \cdots x_n} \)    e.g. {2, 4, 8} → \( (2 \times 4 \times 8)^{1/3} = 4 \)

Weighted Mean

Each value is multiplied by its weight and divided by the total of the weights.

Example: \( \bar{x}_w = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i x_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i} \)    e.g. (10:100, 40:50) → \( \frac{10(100) + 40(50)}{100 + 50} = 20 \)

Harmonic Mean

The harmonic mean is ideal for averaging rates or ratios—such as speeds or financial ratios—where smaller values have a stronger influence.

Example: \( H = \frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{x_i}} \)    e.g. {2, 4, 8} → \( \frac{3}{1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8} \approx 3.429 \)

Quadratic Mean (RMS)

The quadratic mean, or root mean square, measures the magnitude of a set—ideal for values related to energy, power, or signal strength.

Example: \( Q = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i^2} \)    e.g. {2, 4, 9, 10, 12} → \( \sqrt{\frac{2^2 + 4^2 + 9^2 + 10^2 + 12^2}{5}} \approx 8.31 \)

Median

The median represents the middle value of ordered data, dividing the dataset into two equal halves.

Formula (conceptually): sort values \( x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n \), then \[ \text{Median} = \begin{cases} x_{\frac{n+1}{2}}, & n \text{ odd} \\ \frac{x_{\frac{n}{2}} + x_{\frac{n}{2}+1}}{2}, & n \text{ even} \end{cases} \]
Example: {3, 7, 2, 9, 5} → {2, 3, 5, 7, 9} → median = 5

Mode

The mode is the most frequent value in a dataset. Some distributions may be bimodal or multimodal.

Example: {4, 2, 4, 3, 4, 2} → mode = 4

Variance Recurrence Formula

This module lets you explore how variance can be updated efficiently when new data are introduced.

Formula: \( s_{n+1}^2 = \frac{n-1}{n} s_n^2 + \frac{(x_{n+1} - \bar{x}_n)^2}{n+1} \)
Example: if \( s_5^2 = 4 \), \( \bar{x}_5 = 10 \), and \( x_6 = 14 \), then \( s_6^2 = \frac{4(4)}{5} + \frac{(14 - 10)^2}{6} = 6.13 \)

Standard Deviation

The standard deviation measures how spread out values are around the mean.

Formula: \( s = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2} \)
Example: {2, 4, 4, 4, 5, 5, 7, 9} → \( s = 2 \)

Interquartile Range (IQR)

The IQR measures the spread of the middle 50% of the data, resistant to outliers.

Formula: \( IQR = Q_3 - Q_1 \)
Example: {1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13} → \( Q_1 = 3.5, Q_3 = 10 \Rightarrow IQR = 6.5 \)

Choosing the Right Mean

Type Application Considerations
Arithmetic Additive data (e.g. salaries, scores) Sensitive to outliers; most intuitive average
Geometric Multiplicative data (e.g. growth rates, indices) Only valid for positive values; dampens large variations
Weighted Data with unequal importance or reliability Weights must be meaningful and justified
Harmonic Rates and ratios (e.g. speed, precision, financial returns) Gives more weight to smaller values; inappropriate for zero or negative data
Quadratic Physical magnitudes and signal processing (e.g. voltage, error size) Emphasizes larger deviations; always ≥ arithmetic mean
