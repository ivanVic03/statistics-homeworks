2.1 Kolmogorov’s Contribution (1933)

Kolmogorov did not attempt to define what probability is, but rather how it must behave. His axiomatic system separates mathematical structure from philosophical interpretation, giving probability a rigorous foundation within measure theory.

2.2 The Probability Space \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)\)

  • \(\Omega\): Sample space (set of all possible outcomes).
  • \(\mathcal{F}\): A \(\sigma\)-algebra of events (measurable sets).
  • P: A probability measure defined on \(\mathcal{F}\).

2.3 Kolmogorov’s Axioms

Axiom 1 (Non-negativity): \(P(A) \ge 0\).

Axiom 2 (Normalization): \(P(\Omega) = 1\).

Axiom 3 (\(\sigma\)-additivity):

\[ P\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} P(A_i) \qquad\text{for disjoint } A_i. \]

2.4 Resolving the Inconsistencies

The axiomatic approach forces the analyst to specify the probability measure explicitly. In Bertrand’s paradox, the three answers correspond to three different measures:

  • Uniform in angle → \(1/3\)
  • Uniform along a radius → \(1/2\)
  • Uniform over the disk area → \(1/4\)
